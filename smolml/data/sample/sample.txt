The scout walks the idea space the way a careful reader walks a long sentence:
one step at a time, predicting what comes next, and paying attention when the
guess is wrong. That habit is the whole project in miniature. A model that
predicts the next byte well is, by the plainest possible argument, a model that
compresses the text well, because the number of bits you need to store a symbol
is the negative logarithm of the probability you assigned to it. Predict better
and you spend fewer bits. Spend fewer bits and you have, by definition,
predicted better. There is no daylight between the two ideas, and pretending
otherwise only invites confusion.

We measure everything in bits per byte. A model that knows nothing assigns equal
probability to all two hundred and fifty six byte values, which costs eight bits
for every byte it sees. A model that has learned the shape of English text spends
far less, because after the letter q it expects a u, after a period it expects a
space and a capital letter, and after the word the it expects a noun before long.
Each small expectation, when it is correct, shaves a fraction of a bit off the
running total, and those fractions accumulate into the gap between a clever model
and a clueless one.

The trap we are stepping around is the belief that a bigger model is the only
road to a better one. Scaling laws are real and they are humbling: validation
loss falls smoothly as you pour in more parameters, more data, and more compute,
and the curve has been measured carefully enough that nobody serious argues with
it anymore. But the curve describes one family of learners. It does not prove
that this family is the most compute efficient family that could exist. That is
the open question, and it is the question worth a scout, because answering it does
not require a warehouse of accelerators, only a fair scoreboard and a steady hand.

A fair scoreboard counts compute in floating point operations rather than seconds.
Seconds depend on the chip, the kernel, the phase of the moon. Floating point
operations depend only on the arithmetic the algorithm actually performs, so a
matrix multiply costs the same whether it runs on a laptop or a rented cluster.
That is why the harness carries an honest counter that adds up the multiplies and
the adds, charges the backward pass its fair share, and refuses to let any
candidate hide its true cost behind a fast implementation. The counter is the
referee. If the referee is wrong, every match it judges is wrong with it, so we
check its arithmetic by hand and we keep the checks where everyone can read them.

Training proceeds against a fixed budget of operations rather than a fixed number
of steps. We let a model learn until it has spent its allowance, record how low
its validation bits per byte fell, and plot that point against the operations it
burned getting there. Run several budgets and the points become a curve. Lay two
curves on the same axes and the comparison is immediate: at any given budget, the
lower curve is the better learner, and the better learner is the one we keep.

The baseline is a transformer, built plainly and counted honestly. It normalizes
before each sublayer rather than after, scales activations by their root mean
square, and rotates its queries and keys so that position is encoded by angle
rather than by an added vector. Attention is causal, which means a position may
look only at the positions behind it, never ahead, because looking ahead would be
cheating at the very prediction game we are scoring. None of these choices are
exotic; they are simply the quiet conventions that a careful baseline ought to
follow, so that when a candidate beats it we can trust the victory.

What would a real victory look like? Not a clever trick that runs faster on one
machine, and not a model that happens to answer a trivia question. A real victory
is a mechanism whose learning dynamics extract more reduction in loss from each
operation than backpropagation through a transformer manages. Maybe it memorizes
cheaply and saves its expensive gradients for genuine generalization. Maybe it
adapts as it reads, paying for that adaptation out of the same honest budget, and
still comes out ahead. We do not know, and the not knowing is the entire reason to
look. The scout does not promise to find treasure. The scout promises to search
the ground carefully, to mark the dry holes as clearly as the wet ones, and to
leave a map good enough that the next person does not have to dig blind.

So we begin with the boring, load bearing work: a clean data pipeline, a referee
we trust, a baseline we respect, and a leaderboard that cannot be gamed. Only once
that foundation holds do the interesting questions become askable at all. A
question you cannot measure is a daydream. A question you can measure, cheaply and
honestly and as many times as you like, is a research program. The difference
between the two is exactly the harness, and the harness is what we build first.
