## Intuition

A model that assigns probability $p$ to the next byte can be turned into a compressor that stores
that byte in $-\log_2 p$ **bits** (that is what arithmetic coding does), and vice versa. So
*predicting well* and *compressing well* are the **same objective**. There is no difference between
"a language model" and "a compressor" at the level of the math — only in how you package and reuse
the artifact.

## What bits-per-byte means

**bits-per-byte (bpb)** is the average cost, in bits, to encode each byte of held-out text:

$$\mathrm{bpb} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n} -\log_2 p(b_i \mid c_i)$$

It is just cross-entropy measured in bits and normalized per byte. Working at the **byte** level
means no tokenizer choices muddy the comparison. Lower is better:

- random ASCII English &asymp; **4&ndash;5 bpb**,
- a good model on `enwik8` reaches &asymp; **1 bpb**,
- the [Hutter Prize](http://prize.hutter1.net) frontier is &asymp; **0.9 bpb**.

The uninformed anchor is **8 bpb** — a uniform guess over 256 byte values. Every bit you shave off
8 is a byte the model stopped being surprised by.

## Worked example

Suppose after `"the cat sat on the ma"` the model puts $p(\texttt{'t'}) = 0.5$. The cost is
$-\log_2 0.5 = \mathbf{1}$ bit for that byte. If it were more confident, $p(\texttt{'t'}) = 0.9$,
the cost drops to $-\log_2 0.9 \approx \mathbf{0.152}$ bits. Sum these costs over the whole eval
stream and divide by the byte count to get bpb. So "lower bpb" literally means "less surprised,
byte by byte."

<Callout variant="insight">
Chasing low **bpb-per-FLOP** *is* chasing a better predictive model. That equivalence lets us
borrow the entire compression literature — especially
[online context mixing](/concepts/context-mixing) — both as inspiration and as a per-FLOP
**ceiling** to measure candidates against.
</Callout>
