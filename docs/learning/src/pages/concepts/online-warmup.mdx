## Intuition

There are two ways to spend FLOPs *before* you predict the eval stream — and a cheap third one the
cold reference forgoes:

- **Amortized** (the transformer): pour compute into *pretraining weights* on the prior corpus, then
  predict with a frozen model.
- **Transductive** (the [context-mixing reference](/concepts/context-mixing)): pretrain *nothing*;
  learn entirely online, from a **cold start**, on the eval stream itself.
- **Warm-started** (the third thing): a transductive learner streams the freely-usable prior corpus
  through its *own* online update first — warming its state — then carries that state into eval. Its
  FLOPs are counted like any other, but they are absurdly cheap next to gradient pretraining.

[Prequential evaluation](/concepts/prequential-evaluation) (ADR-0004) puts all three on one curve.
The **transductive handicap** is what the cold reference suffers by skipping that warm-up: it begins
the eval stream knowing nothing, pays ~8 bits on byte 1, and climbs the learning curve from scratch.
That handicap — *not* the mechanism — is why the cheap online learner loses to the transformer.
Remove it and it wins (see [B.2](/experiments/B.2-warmed-mixing)).

## The math (why warming is nearly free and very effective)

A context model's prediction is a smoothed frequency over the $V = 256$ bytes — add-$\alpha$
(Laplace) over the counts for the current context:

$$p(b \mid \text{ctx}) = \frac{\text{count}[\text{ctx}][b] + \alpha}{\sum_{b'} \text{count}[\text{ctx}][b'] + \alpha V}$$

Cold, every $\text{count}$ is zero, so the first predictions are ~uniform (the specialist abstains)
and the learner pays the full ~8-bit toll until the tables fill. **Warming on $N$ prior bytes** fills
those tables with real frequencies, so the *first* eval byte is already predicted from thousands of
prior observations. The cost is just $N$ online updates — for byte-level mixing, $\approx O(K \cdot V)$
FLOPs each, with **no backprop and no matmul-heavy forward/backward**:

$$C_{\text{warm}} \approx N \cdot O(K \cdot V) \quad\text{vs.}\quad C_{\text{pretrain}} \approx 6 \cdot N_{\text{params}} \cdot D$$

On real `enwik8`, warming a few hundred-thousand bytes turns a ~3.2-bpb cold start into ~2.8 bpb at a
tiny FLOP cost — while a transformer needs **orders of magnitude** more compute to reach comparable
bpb. That is the whole curve below: same mechanism, warmed.

## Why it's a legitimate Source-(iv) move

Warm-start is *amortized pretraining for a transductive learner*. It is allowed — ADR-0004 lists
"online warm-up" as in-scope and the prior corpus is freely usable — and it is **honestly metered**:
every warm-up byte's FLOPs are on the curve, so it can never hide compute the way an uncounted eval
pass would.

It qualifies under the [Source-(iv)](/concepts/source-iv-advantage) filter because the *dynamics* —
cheap online frequency estimation — extract far more loss-reduction per FLOP than gradient descent
does at this scale. It is the cleanest demonstration of the project's thesis that **how** you spend
FLOPs matters more than **how many**.

<Callout variant="insight">
The cheap online learner was never the problem — its *cold start* was. A FLOP-counted warm-up is the
difference between a yardstick the transformer beats and a candidate that beats the transformer.
</Callout>

<Callout variant="caveat">
Warming is not a free lunch you can pour forever: it moves the learner to its single-pass frequency
ceiling, not past it. B.2's [gated escalation](/experiments/B.2-warmed-mixing) shows the *next* lever
— adaptive-depth mixing — does **not** pay, because the warmed mixer is already near its per-FLOP
frontier. Warm-start banks the win; it does not keep giving.
</Callout>
