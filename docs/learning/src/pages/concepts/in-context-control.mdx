## Intuition — the biology

A bacterium foraging for food cannot see the gradient. It senses only the concentration *where it
currently is*. So it does something almost embarrassingly simple: it swims a little, checks whether
things got better, and biases its motion toward "better" — a **run** — reorienting with a **tumble**
when things got worse. With essentially no memory, run-and-tumble *chemotaxis* climbs to a source
and keeps tracking it as the source drifts away.

That is the whole idea of this rung, abstracted to a 1-D ring. It is also a different *kind* of task
from everything else in this compendium: the earlier pages ask a model to **predict** the next byte;
here the model must **act**. It is the top rung of a graded in-context-learning suite — recall, then
rule, then composition, then **control** — and the only one that scores *predict + act* together.

<Callout variant="note" title="what makes it “in-context”">
The model is never trained on the specific drift it will face at eval time. It has to figure out
how to climb and track *within the episode*, from the feedback on its own tape — no weight update,
no gradient. The behaviour is learned in-context, the way a few-shot prompt teaches a frozen model
a new task.
</Callout>

## The environment — `ChemoEnv`

A ring of $W = 16$ cells carries a smooth concentration bump that peaks at position $\mu$. The
concentration a cell reads off is a Gaussian in the ring distance to the peak, then quantized to
$L = 8$ levels — the symbol the agent actually senses:

$$\text{conc}(x) = \exp\!\left(-\,\frac{d_{\text{ring}}(x, \mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right), \qquad \text{sensed token} = \big\lfloor (L-1)\cdot \text{conc}(x) + \tfrac12 \big\rfloor$$

Two facts make it hard. First, the peak **drifts** every step (each episode has its own speed and
direction), so a fixed position rots — the agent must *keep* adapting. Second, the agent at position
$p$ senses **only** `conc(p)` — one local reading, exactly like the bacterium. Its actions are
`LEFT`, `STAY`, `RIGHT`, one cell at a time.

The decisive design choice: **obs ≡ reward ≡ the concentration you now smell.** There is no separate
reward channel to game; doing well *is* standing where it smells strong.

<Callout variant="insight" title="held-out by construction">
Training and evaluation draw drift rates from **disjoint pools**. The eval episodes are unseen
dynamics, so a model cannot win by memorizing a trajectory — it has to have learned the *strategy*.
The rung is memorization-proof by design.
</Callout>

## The interaction — a token tape

Everything happens on one interleaved tape of senses and actions:

`c₀ · a₀ · c₁ · a₁ · c₂ · a₂ · …`

The model reads a concentration token, emits an **action** (its policy is the logits over the action
sub-vocabulary), the environment applies the move and the drift, and returns the next sensed
concentration. The same model also **predicts** the next concentration token — that prediction is its
**world model** of a sensor it only partly controls. Policy and world model are learned jointly, on
the same tape, through the same FLOP-honest `step` channel the byte models use, so **every rollout
FLOP is counted** the same way bits-per-byte counts prediction FLOPs.

## The metric — regret, and world-model bits

The headline is **regret against an oracle** that can see $\mu$. Regret is the reward the agent
leaves on the table — the gap between the oracle's mean per-step reward and the agent's — reported
**per total training FLOP** at **fixed parameters**. Lower is better; the oracle sits at $\approx 0$.

$$\text{regret} = \bar{r}_{\text{oracle}} - \bar{r}_{\text{agent}}, \qquad \bar{r} = \frac{1}{H}\sum_{t=1}^{H} r_t$$

This is the [loss-per-FLOP](/concepts/loss-per-flop-and-scaling-laws) discipline with the *same*
x-axis as every other page (total FLOPs, log scale) and a control quantity on the y-axis instead of
bpb. The **secondary** read is the **world-model bits** — how well the model predicts the sensor it
does not fully control, $-\log_2 p_\theta(c_{t+1}\mid \text{tape}_{\le t})$.

<Callout variant="caveat">
World-model bits are **policy-conditional**: a different policy visits different cells and therefore
faces a different prediction problem, so the number is *not* comparable across policies. Read it only
as a within-policy diagnostic — never as a leaderboard against a model that moved differently.
</Callout>

## How a transformer learns it — Algorithm Distillation

A transformer cannot do in-context control out of the box; left untrained it just emits noise. So we
**distill** the behaviour. Roll an adaptive source — plain run-and-tumble — across many *fresh*
episodes, record the tapes, and train the transformer to predict the next token over the whole tape
(policy and world model jointly). Then evaluate on **held-out** episodes. In-context climb-and-track
emerges: the network has absorbed the *algorithm*, not any one trajectory.

## The bar — what it costs, what it buys

The transformer baseline, held at **fixed 148,608 parameters**, on the drifting held-out eval, as we
pour in more distillation:

| distillation steps | total FLOPs | regret ↓ | reward ↑ |
| ---: | ---: | ---: | ---: |
| 150  | 2.97 × 10¹¹ | 0.229 | 0.704 |
| 600  | 1.19 × 10¹² | 0.171 | 0.762 |
| 1500 | 2.96 × 10¹² | 0.141 | 0.792 |

For scale: a random policy floors at $\approx 0.37$ mean reward, and the $\mu$-seeing oracle sits at
$\approx 0$ regret. Within a single episode the trained model's **second-half reward beats its
first-half** — the signature of *climb, then track* you can watch in the rollout above.

<Callout variant="insight">
Regret falls and reward rises monotonically with compute — the loss-per-FLOP discipline carried
intact from byte prediction to **control**. The one curve the whole compendium reads still reads:
more learning per FLOP, now measured by *predict + act*.
</Callout>

## The candidates — three minimal organisms vs the bar

Three minimal-organism candidates challenged the bar. Only one beat it — and *how* it won is the rung's

- **[C.A.1 — reservoir, frozen readout](/experiments/C.A.1-reservoir-control):** a fixed echo-state core with a distilled linear readout. Regret falls with compute but **caps at 0.278**, above the bar's 0.229. **Loses.**
- **[C.A.1b — reservoir + plastic readout](/experiments/C.A.1-reservoir-control):** the same core with an **online** reward-modulated readout that learns *within the episode* at ~0 distillation. It clears the random floor with genuine [Source-(iv)](/concepts/source-iv-advantage) dynamics, but its regret (0.501) is ~2.2× the bar's. **Loses on regret** — a distinct ultra-cheap / modest-skill regime.
- **[C.A.2 — chemotaxis_min](/experiments/C.A.2-chemotaxis-min-control):** five hand-coded run-and-tumble scalars. **0.180 regret at $2.70\times10^{5}$ FLOPs** — lower than the bar at ~6 orders of magnitude fewer FLOPs. **Beats the bar, on the FLOP axis.**

<Callout variant="insight" title="only the floor beats the bar — and that is the finding">
Of three candidates, **only `chemotaxis_min`** reaches the lower-left, and it does so by being the rung's
**hand-coded stationary optimum** at ~zero compute. The reservoir family clears the random floor and shows
real learning dynamics, but a fixed reservoir + linear readout underperforms the transformer's learned
representation on regret. It is the [Pareto-hollow reflex](/experiments/first-finding-pareto) carried onto
control: **genuine online learning is real but modest here, while a cheap fixed reaction dominates the
per-FLOP metric on a *stationary* rung.** Always plot the floor first — which is exactly why the next
section reads the win as a *limitation*, not a refutation.
</Callout>

## Read honestly — what this rung does not yet show

v1 distills a **stationary reactive** source. Run-and-tumble's optimal reaction is the *same* for
every drift rate — climb the local gradient — so the agent never has to infer how fast the world is
moving. That keeps the rung clean (leakage- and memorization-proof, and a genuine demonstration of
climb-and-track on held-out dynamics) but it also means the rung does **not yet isolate** in-context
*drift-rate inference* from a fixed reaction.

<Callout variant="caveat">
Frame the result as "**learns to climb and track held-out dynamics from local feedback**," not "infers
the drift." Distinguishing a clever fixed reaction from genuine in-episode inference needs a source
that itself *learns within the episode* — the documented next step for this rung. The [C.A.2
chemotaxis_min result](/experiments/C.A.2-chemotaxis-min-control) sharpens exactly this: a stationary,
hand-coded reactor already takes the rung's per-FLOP optimum, so a harder "C.A.0b" rung — one whose
distillation source *learns within the episode* — is what would force genuine drift-rate inference.
</Callout>

A **sibling rung** takes a step in exactly that direction: the [forage contextual bandit
(C.A.4)](/experiments/C.A.4-forage-local-learners) is **reflex-proof** — no fixed policy is
near-optimal — and there an 8-parameter local learner that *genuinely adapts within the episode*
still beats the swept distilled transformer on regret at ~6 orders of magnitude fewer FLOPs. On that
rung the win is genuine within-episode learning rather than a fixed-reaction floor, and the per-FLOP
lever is **structure** (exact per-cue-type credit assignment), not capacity.
