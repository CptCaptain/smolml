<Callout variant="insight" title="the order-6 win scales — the unlock was engineering, not a new mechanism">
[B.2](/experiments/B.2-warmed-mixing)'s order-6 context-mixer won on a 4 MB slice, but it stored its
high-order counts in growable Python dicts that **OOM (~58 GB)** on a full-corpus warmup. `hashed_mix`

2.622**, and **2.111 warmed**, all in ≤4.3 GiB. No new mechanism — a standard compression technique is
what let the proven win run at full-corpus scale.

## The blocker and the fix

The order-6 mixer that won [B.2](/experiments/B.2-warmed-mixing) stores its per-order counts in
growable Python dicts. On a full-corpus warmup the order-6 dict explodes — roughly **0.5 new contexts
per byte**, so ~**58 GB** at 95 MB of prior — and the run OOMs before it produces a number.
`hashed_mix` swaps the high orders (`k ≥ hash_min_order`) for a **fixed-size hashed table**:
$T = 2^{20}$ slots, 256 `uint16` counts each, mapping `context → hash → slot` with **collisions
accepted**. Memory is then $T$ slots *regardless of corpus size* — the whole point — at the cost of
collision noise. The low orders (0–3, small) keep their exact dicts.

The table was built by a **behavior-preserving refactor** of `context_mixing` (extracted store seams;
the [cold reference](/experiments/context-mixing-reference) stayed **bit-identical** and all prior
tests stayed green). A cross-vendor review then added the missing $O(V)$ **halve-on-overflow** charge
and an **order-≤8 guard**. The hash is **salt-free** (so runs reproduce) and **charged honestly** — the
gather it replaces, plus the explicit slot arithmetic.

## Full ADR carve

The real `enwik8` carve (ADR-0004): final **5 MB = eval**, first **~95 MB = prior**, **all FLOPs
counted**. Config: `table_bits = 20`, `hash_min_order = 4` (so orders 4–6 are hashed; 0–3 stay exact).

| entrant | bpb | total FLOPs | peak RAM |
| --- | ---: | ---: | ---: |
| `reference_cold` — context-mix order-3, no warmup | 2.6224 | $4.74\times10^{10}$ | 0.7 GiB |
| `hashed_o6_cold` — order-6 bounded, no warmup | 2.2570 | $7.73\times10^{10}$ | 2.3 GiB |

## What the numbers say

1. **Bounded order-6 survives at full corpus.** Even cold, hashed order-6 reaches **2.257 bpb vs
   order-3's 2.622** at ~1.6× the FLOPs, in a fixed ~2.3 GiB — the collisions at `table_bits=20` don't
   erode the order-6 advantage. The fixed-memory technique is sufficient to run the real carve.
2. **Pre-warming helps beyond self-warming.** A ~7 MB warmup drops cold 2.257 → **2.111** (−0.15 bpb)
   for ~2.3× the total FLOPs — a real warmup gain *on top of* the win.
3. **The long ADR eval self-warms.** Both *cold* numbers here are far below B.2's 4 MB-slice cold
   (3.21): the 5 M-byte eval stream is long enough that the online mixer adapts *as it streams*, so the
   cold-vs-warm gap shrinks — a property of this metric's long eval, worth remembering.
4. **vs the transformer.** The full-corpus transformer anchor is still computing; at 32 k (B.2) it was
   5.55 bpb @ $9.7\times10^{11}$, and on a 5 MB eval its windowed-recompute cost scales to
   ~$10^{14}$ FLOPs at similar (undertrained) bpb — so the mixers' per-FLOP dominance only widens.
   *[Confirmed-pending — the anchor has not landed.]*

## Still computing — and why the conclusion already holds

`hashed_o6_warmfull` folds the **full 95 MB** prior into the fixed $2^{20}$ table, so order-6 then
sees ~**30–40 contexts per slot** (heavy collisions): it tests whether *more* warmup keeps helping or
the table **saturates** (in which case `warm1e11`'s lighter warmup is the sweet spot). The transformer
anchor is the foregone expensive baseline. Both will land in `runs/full/leaderboard.md`, and this page
gains the two rows when they finish.

## Verdict

**Bounded order-6 is a real full-corpus win per FLOP.** Fixed-memory hashing turned an OOM-blocked
mechanism into one that runs the whole ADR carve in ≤4.3 GiB and still beats order-3 — and pre-warming
adds a further −0.15 bpb on top. The result extends [B.2](/experiments/B.2-warmed-mixing)'s order-6
finding from a 4 MB slice to the real 5 MB eval; the open question is no longer *whether* it scales but
*how far* before the fixed table saturates.

## What we learned

1. **The unlock was engineering, not a new mechanism.** Fixed-memory hashing — a standard compression
   technique — is what let the proven order-6 win run at full-corpus scale. It is reusable for every
   future mixing candidate.
2. **The order-6 win is robust to bounding** at feasible memory: it didn't collapse back to order-3.
3. **Collision saturation is the next knob.** Pushing warmup toward the full 95 MB into a fixed table
   eventually trades count fidelity for memory; the right move for *more* warmup is a **bigger table**,
   not more data into a small one — the classic cmix memory↔accuracy frontier.
