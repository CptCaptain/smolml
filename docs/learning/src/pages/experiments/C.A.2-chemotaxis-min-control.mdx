<Callout variant="insight" title="lower regret at ~6 OOM fewer FLOPs — the only candidate to beat the bar">
`chemotaxis_min` reaches **0.180 regret at $2.70\times10^{5}$ total FLOPs** with **5 parameters**. The
[transformer bar](/concepts/in-context-control)'s cheapest point is **0.229 at $2.97\times10^{11}$** with
148,608 params — so chemotaxis_min is **lower-regret at ~6 orders of magnitude fewer FLOPs**. At any FLOP
budget the transformer can reach, it already sits in the lower-left; the transformer only pushes regret to
0.141 by spending $2.96\times10^{12}$ FLOPs — about **7 orders of magnitude** more.
</Callout>

<Callout variant="caveat" title="this is a FLOP-floor win on a stationary rung — do not overclaim">
The [C.A.0 rung](/concepts/in-context-control)'s optimal policy is a **stationary reactive
run-and-tumble**: a rate-agnostic reactor clears every held-out drift rate, because the rung does **not yet
isolate** in-context drift-*rate* inference. `chemotaxis_min` **is** that hand-coded run-and-tumble —
essentially the **distillation source itself** — so it nails the rung at ~zero cost while the transformer
pays ~7 OOM more FLOPs to *approximately learn the same fixed reaction*. Read this as "**the rung's cheap
optimum exposed**," **not** "minimal organisms beat transformers at control in general." It strengthens the
documented case for a within-episode-*learner* source (a "C.A.0b") that would demand genuine in-episode
inference and re-open the gap.
</Callout>

## Intuition — a bacterium with almost no machinery

A bacterium does [chemotaxis](/concepts/in-context-control) with essentially no hardware: it **integrates**
the concentration it is smelling, asks "am I improving?", keeps its heading while the signal **rises**, and
**reverses** (tumbles) when it **drops**. The "in-context adaptation" is the **integrator state** — a leaky
baseline tracking the local gradient — *not* weight learning. So the whole controller is a handful of
learnable scalars plus a few pointwise operations per step, and its total FLOPs are dominated by the (very
cheap) eval rollout.

## The math — five scalars

`chemotaxis_min` has **5 learnable scalars**: leak $\lambda$, gain $g$, `stay_bias`, `climb`, and
`sharpness`. Per step, with sensed concentration $c$ and leaky baseline $b$:

$$s = c - b \quad (\text{surprise}), \qquad b \leftarrow (1-\lambda)\,b + \lambda\,c \quad (\text{integrator update})$$

The action logits are **run-and-tumble around the current heading**: *keep* $= g\cdot s$ on the last
direction, *reverse* $= -g\cdot s$, and *STAY* $= \texttt{stay\_bias}$. So a rising signal ($s &gt; 0$)
reinforces the current heading and a falling signal flips it — exactly run-and-tumble. The `climb` and
`sharpness` scalars shape the world-model head (a peak placed one level up-gradient) and the logit
temperature. Everything is pointwise.

<Callout variant="note" title="FLOP honesty">
Every op is charged via `pointwise_flops`: $26 + 5L$ per step $\approx$ **66** at $L = 8$ levels. The
cross-vendor review corrected this count **up** from an initial under-count, to include the compares, the
where-selects, and the logit construction. `num_params = 5`. There is no matvec and no backward pass to
speak of — the cost floor is the rollout itself.
</Callout>

## The counterintuitive twist — distillation makes it *worse*

Distilling `chemotaxis_min` (CE on the next token) **raises** its regret, because cross-entropy optimizes
next-token **prediction** at the expense of **reward-optimality** — and the cheapest point is already
reward-optimal:

| distillation steps | regret ↓ |
| ---: | ---: |
| 0   | **0.180** |
| 100 | 0.191 |
| 400 | 0.251 |

Meanwhile the **world model improves** under distillation (world-model bits fall **4.37 → 2.04** from 0 to
400 steps) — prediction gets sharper exactly as control gets worse. The two objectives pull apart, and on
this rung the **zero-distillation, reward-optimal point is the one to keep**.

## Verdict — BEATS THE BAR (on the FLOP axis), with the caveat

`chemotaxis_min` is the **only** Space-C candidate to beat the transformer bar: lower regret at ~6 OOM
fewer FLOPs and 5 vs 148,608 params. But it is a **FLOP-floor** result — the hand-coded optimum of a
*stationary* rung — so it is honest evidence for loss-per-FLOP and a sharp pointer at the rung's limit,
**not** a claim that minimal organisms out-control transformers in general.

## What we learned

1. **The rung has a cheap optimum, and we just found it.** On a stationary run-and-tumble task the optimal
   policy is a fixed reaction; five hand-tuned scalars *are* that reaction, so they take the per-FLOP metric
   outright while the transformer spends ~7 OOM more FLOPs to approximate the same thing.
2. **Distillation ≠ improvement when the objectives diverge.** CE-distillation sharpened prediction
   (world-model bits 4.37 → 2.04) while *degrading* reward — a clean reminder that the world-model bits are
   a within-policy diagnostic, never the control objective.
3. **Plot the floor first — the lesson generalizes from bytes to control.** Just as a free frequency model
   exposed the [first finding](/experiments/first-finding-pareto)'s Pareto-hollow win, the cheap fixed
   reaction exposes how much of this rung is *stationary*. The fix is a harder rung: a within-episode-learner
   distillation source (a "C.A.0b") that demands genuine in-context drift-rate inference and would re-open
   the gap the transformer currently pays so much to cross.
