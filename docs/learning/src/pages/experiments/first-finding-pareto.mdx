## What was run

All three models on the **same** prequential protocol ‚Äî synthetic text8 clone (200000 bytes,
seed 0), final 512 bytes as the eval stream, `context_window=512`, slow-core `d_model=48,
n_layers=3` ‚Äî swept across pretraining budgets $\{0, 2\times10^{9}, 10^{10}, 4\times10^{10}\}$
FLOPs, scored as validation bits-per-byte vs **total** FLOPs (pretrain $+$ inference).

## The finding (honest)

`fast_weight` (frozen transformer core $+$ online associative memory) beats the transformer
**baseline** at low budgets (‚àí0.59 at b0, ‚àí0.23 at b2e9) and **loses** once the core is well-trained
(+0.14 at b1e10, +0.20 at b4e10) ‚Äî the memory helps a weak core and hurts a good one.

But the win is **Pareto-hollow**: the free online context-mixing reference reaches 4.78 bpb for
$\sim4.3\times10^{6}$ FLOPs ‚Äî ~1000&times; cheaper than either neural model and *lower* bpb than
both until $\sim10^{10}$ FLOPs. So `fast_weight`'s wins over the transformer all happen in the
regime a free frequency model already dominates, and it gives up its edge exactly where neural
models finally beat the reference. A real-`enwik8` control reproduced the pattern.

<Callout variant="insight" title="‚Äúbeats the baseline‚Äù ‚âÝ ‚Äúgood per FLOP‚Äù">
The headline "fast-weight beats the transformer at low budgets" is a race between **two runners
both behind a free pedestrian.** Once the free reference is on the axis, beating the baseline is no
longer evidence of a per-FLOP win. **Every candidate from here is plotted against that free floor,
not just against the transformer.** That is the methodological lesson worth internalizing.
</Callout>

**Verdict:** the Source-(iv) "free memorization" thesis is **not supported on this data** ‚Äî the
memory behaves as a weak always-on frequency model, not rote recall. (Full breakdown, variance, and
the `enwik8` control: [A.1](/experiments/A.1-fast-weight-memory).)
