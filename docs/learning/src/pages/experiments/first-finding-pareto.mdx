## What was run

All three models on the **same** prequential protocol — synthetic text8 clone (200000 bytes,
seed 0), final 512 bytes as the eval stream, `context_window=512`, slow-core `d_model=48,
n_layers=3, seq_len=128` (identical to the canonical 0.2 / A.1 runs) — swept across pretraining budgets $\{0, 2\times10^{9}, 10^{10}, 4\times10^{10}\}$
FLOPs, scored as validation bits-per-byte vs **total** FLOPs (pretrain $+$ inference).

## The finding (honest)

`fast_weight` (frozen transformer core $+$ online associative memory) beats the transformer
**baseline** at every budget where the core is weak — −0.59 at b0, −0.30 at b2e9, −0.10 at b1e10 —
and **loses** once the core is well-trained: +0.19 at b4e10. The memory helps a weak core and hurts
a good one.

But the win is **Pareto-hollow**: the free online context-mixing reference reaches 4.78 bpb for
$\sim4.3\times10^{6}$ FLOPs — **40&times;&ndash;2300&times; cheaper** than the neural runs through
$10^{10}$, and *lower bpb than both* at every budget through $10^{10}$ (where the neural models are
still at 5.9&ndash;6.0). The
neural models overtake the free reference only at $4\times10^{10}$ — exactly the budget where
`fast_weight` gives up its edge and loses to the plain transformer. So every `fast_weight` win over
the transformer happens inside the region a free frequency model already dominates, and the
memory's value vanishes precisely where neural compute finally pays off. A real-`enwik8` control
reproduced the pattern.

<Callout variant="insight" title="“beats the baseline” ≠ “good per FLOP”">
The headline "fast-weight beats the transformer at low budgets" is a race between **two runners
both behind a free pedestrian.** Once the free reference is on the axis, beating the baseline is no
longer evidence of a per-FLOP win. **Every candidate from here is plotted against that free floor,
not just against the transformer.** That is the methodological lesson worth internalizing.
</Callout>

**Verdict:** the Source-(iv) "free memorization" thesis is **not supported on this data** — the
memory behaves as a weak always-on frequency model, not rote recall. (Full breakdown, variance, and
the `enwik8` control: [A.1](/experiments/A.1-fast-weight-memory).)
