## Where it came from

The [B.1](/experiments/B.1-surprise-gated-pc-refinement) design fan-out's adversarial critics noticed
(and a verification probe confirmed) that the
[context-mixing reference](/experiments/context-mixing-reference) loses to the transformer **only
because it cold-starts** — it is purely [transductive](/concepts/online-warmup) and throws away
everything it could learn from the freely-usable prior corpus. That is a *handicap*, not a property of
the mechanism. Remove it cheaply and the cheap learner wins.

## Phase 1 — `warm_mix`: remove the transductive handicap

`warm_mix` is the context-mixer with **one** new idea: a **stateful prior&rarr;eval handoff**. During
pretraining it folds the prior corpus into its count tables (warm-up, **every FLOP counted**), then
**deep-copies** that warmed state into each eval stream so prediction starts from warmed statistics
instead of cold uniform priors. At warm-up budget 0 it is **bit-identical** to the cold reference (a
clean baseline). No leakage: warm-up sees only the carve-disjoint prior; each eval folds into its own
copy.

Real `enwik8` (first 4 MB; eval = final 32,768 bytes, disjoint; all FLOPs counted):

| run | bpb | total FLOPs |
| --- | ---: | ---: |
| transformer (anchor) | 5.5453 | $9.71\times10^{11}$ |
| `warm_mix` cold (= reference) | 3.2106 | $3.05\times10^{8}$ |
| `warm_mix` warmed @1e9 | 2.8805 | $1.30\times10^{9}$ |
| **`warm_mix` warmed @1e10** | **2.7700** | $\mathbf{1.03\times10^{10}}$ |

`warm_mix` **strictly dominates the transformer**: lower bpb at **~94&times; fewer total FLOPs**, with
warm-up monotonic (&minus;0.44 bpb cold&rarr;warm). Honest caveats: the tiny transformer is badly
undertrained at this budget (real `enwik8` &gt;&gt; the synthetic clone) and its windowed-recompute
*eval alone* is $9.5\times10^{11}$ ($\approx$3000&times; `warm_mix`'s eval); at much larger scale it
would eventually win absolute bpb, but **per FLOP at every tractable budget, warmed mixing wins**.
This is the Hutter/PAQ thesis confirmed on real text: a transformer is a FLOP-inefficient *learner* at
tiny scale.

<Callout variant="insight" title="the bar moved">
This is the new bar. Future candidates must beat `warm_mix` (&approx;2.66 bpb @ ~$1.5\times10^{9}$ on
this carve), **not** the transformer. The cold reference is no longer a ceiling a candidate "fails to
reach" — it is a candidate that *wins*, once you stop handicapping it.
</Callout>

## The order curve (what motivated Phase 2)

On real `enwik8`, deeper context keeps paying — unlike the order-0 synthetic clone where it *hurt*:

| `max_order` (warmed @1e9) | 2 | 3 | 4 | 5 | 6 | 8 |
| --- | ---: | ---: | ---: | ---: | ---: | ---: |
| bpb | 3.248 | 2.881 | 2.710 | 2.667 | **2.655** | 2.664 |

But the fixed-order mixer evaluates **all** orders on **every** byte, while the high orders earn bits
only on the minority of bytes with deep recurring context. That looked like a
[Source-(iv)](/concepts/source-iv-advantage) lever: spend the deep-order compute only where it pays.

## Phase 2 — `gated_mix`: the A&cap;C fusion (gated order escalation)

`gated_mix` holds orders `0..K_max` (warmed) but predicts by **escalating cheapest-first** and
**stopping** once a **pre-reveal `1 − max p` gate** says the partial mix is confident enough —
charging FLOPs for only the orders evaluated. This is simultaneously **A** (the expensive high-order
compute fires only on high-surprise bytes) and **C** (an order is "grown into" the mix only where it
pays). At `min_order == max_order` it is bit-identical to fixed-order `warm_mix`.

| entrant | bpb | total FLOPs | eval FLOPs |
| --- | ---: | ---: | ---: |
| fixed `warm_mix` order-2 | 3.2482 | $1.229\times10^{9}$ | $2.44\times10^{8}$ |
| fixed `warm_mix` order-3 | 2.8805 | $1.304\times10^{9}$ | $3.10\times10^{8}$ |
| fixed `warm_mix` order-4 | 2.7096 | $1.351\times10^{9}$ | $3.73\times10^{8}$ |
| fixed `warm_mix` order-5 | 2.6666 | $1.422\times10^{9}$ | $4.32\times10^{8}$ |
| fixed `warm_mix` order-6 | **2.6552** | $1.477\times10^{9}$ | $4.87\times10^{8}$ |
| `gated_mix` thr=0.1 (near-full) | 2.6698 | $1.570\times10^{9}$ | $6.11\times10^{8}$ |

<Callout variant="caveat" title="Pareto-hollow — this is NOT a win">
Every gated point is dominated by a fixed-order point (&le; bpb **and** &le; FLOPs). The mechanism is
clear: the gate **recomputes a confidence softmax at each escalation level** ($O(\text{depth}\cdot V)$
overhead), but the underlying mix is *already* a single cheap $O(K\cdot V)$ matmul — so escalating deep
costs **more** than fixed (thr=0.1: worse bpb *and* more FLOPs), and gating hard degrades bpb faster
than FLOPs drop (the aggressive points lose even with a hypothetically free gate). The B.1 critics
predicted exactly this: the mixer's online weights already do soft marginal-loss allocation, so hard
gating cannot beat it — and the gate is not free.
</Callout>

## FLOP honesty

Both candidates charge every op via `smolml.flops` and return it from `step` / `train_step` (warm-up
folds, mixing, Laplace, mixer-SGD, and — for `gated_mix` — the per-escalation gate arithmetic). A
cross-vendor review caught and fixed an uncharged warm-up-CE recomputation in `warm_mix`; it verified
`gated_mix`'s per-byte charge equals exactly the evaluated orders, the budget guard is a safe upper
bound, the gate is pre-reveal, and `min_order == max_order` is bit-identical to `warm_mix`.

## Verdict

**`warm_mix` is the first real per-FLOP win** and the new bar (the transformer is decisively beaten;
the cold reference is strictly extended). **`gated_mix` is an honest Pareto-hollow** against it —
online mixing is already near its per-FLOP frontier, and adaptive-depth gating doesn't pay because the
thing being gated is already cheap and the gate is not free.

## What we learned

1. **The handicap, not the mechanism.** The context-mixing reference lost to the transformer purely
   from its cold-start; a cheap, FLOP-counted [warm-start](/concepts/online-warmup) flips it to a
   decisive per-FLOP win. The cheap online learner was never the problem.
2. **The corpus matters.** Real `enwik8` rewards deep context (to ~order-6); the order-0 synthetic
   clone punished it. Levers that need genuine difficulty/structure (B.1's gate, B.2's escalation) can
   only be judged on real text — which is why we moved here.
3. **Gating a cheap thing rarely pays.** When the base computation is already a single cheap matmul
   with learned soft allocation, a per-byte gate adds overhead comparable to what it saves. Gating
   wins only when the gated work is *expensive* relative to the gate.
4. **The bar moved.** Future candidates must beat `warm_mix` (&approx;2.66 bpb @ ~$1.5\times10^{9}$ on
   this carve), not the transformer. That likely needs a mechanism capturing structure the mixer
   **cannot** (long-range / positional / semantic) at genuinely low FLOP — not a re-allocation of the
   mixer's own already-efficient compute.
